Why freelancers should always quote with a buffer

Most freelance projects run over their initial time estimate — not because of poor planning, but because client feedback rounds, scope clarifications, and "just one small change" requests are the norm rather than the exception. Quoting your exact estimated hours with no buffer means every one of these normal occurrences eats directly into your profit margin, effectively lowering your real hourly rate below what you intended to charge.

A contingency buffer of 10-20% built into the quote upfront protects against this without needing to renegotiate mid-project, which clients tend to find frustrating. Similarly, factoring in a rush multiplier when a client needs faster turnaround compensates you fairly for the reduced flexibility and potential after-hours work that tight deadlines usually require.

How much buffer should I add for a new client I haven't worked with before?

Consider leaning toward the higher end (15-20%) for new clients, since you don't yet have a feel for their revision patterns or communication style — both of which affect how much extra time a project actually takes.

Should I show the buffer as a separate line item to the client?

This is a business decision, not a technical one — some freelancers fold it into the total quietly, others list it as "contingency" or "revisions allowance" for transparency. Either approach is common in practice.
